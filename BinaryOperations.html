BINARY OPERATIONS



I believe that you have a good knowledge of Set theory.
If not, you need to study Set theory to have a good understanding of Binary Operations

Let's begin with the definition of Binary Operations

A Binary Operation is any rule of combination of any two elements of a non-empty set.
It is usually denoted by an asterisk or small circle( \( * \) or \( \circ \) )

A Binary Operation \( * \) on a non-empty set \( X \) is a function

\( * : X \times X \to X \)

That is, a binary operation is a rule that combines two elements
of a set to produce another element in the same set.

For example, in the set of integers \( \mathbb{Z} \) , Addition, Subtraction, and Multiplication are binary operations on \( \mathbb{Z} \)

Note : From the definition of Binary operations, we see that the set on which a binary operation is defined must be non-empty.


Properties Of Binary Operations



(1) CLOSURE PROPERTY : A non-empty set \( X \) is said to be closed under a binary operation \( * \) if for all \( x,y \in X , x * y \in X \)

That is, if we combine any two elements in the set with the rule of the binary operation, the result must also be an element in that set.

For example, we already mentioned above that \( \mathbb{Z} \) is closed under Addition, Subtraction, and Multiplication.
But what about Division? Let's take a look at the example below

Consider the set of integers \( \mathbb{Z} \)
Let \( 5,8 \in \mathbb{Z} \)
Addition : \( 5 + 8 = 13 \in \mathbb{Z} \)
Subtraction: \( 5-8 = -3 \in \mathbb{Z} \)
Multiplication: \( 5 \times 8 = 40 \in \mathbb{Z} \)
Division: \( \displaystyle{ {5 \over 8} \notin \mathbb{Z} } \)

This shows that \( \mathbb{Z} \) is not closed under Division.


Consider the set of positive integers \( \mathbb{Z^+} \)
Let \( 4,15 \in \mathbb{Z^+} \)
Addition : \( 4 + 15 = 19 \in \mathbb{Z^+} \)
Subtraction: \( 4-15 = -11 \notin \mathbb{Z^+} \)
Multiplication: \( 4 \times 15 = 60 \in \mathbb{Z^+} \)
Division: \( \displaystyle{ {4 \over 15} \notin \mathbb{Z^+} } \)

This shows that \( \mathbb{Z^+} \) is not closed under Subtraction and Division.


(2)COMMUTATIVE PROPERTY : Let \( X \) be a non-empty set which is closed under a binary operation \( * \).
If for all \( x,y \in X \)
\( x * y = y * x \), then the binary operation \( * \) is said to be commutatiive.

That is, if the order of combination of any two elements of the set is immaterial, then \( * \) is commutative


(3)ASSOCIATIVE PROPERTY : Let \( * \) be a binary operation on a non-empty set \( X \).
Suppose \( X \) is closed and commtative under \( * \). That is
for all \( x,y \in X, x*y \in X \) and \( x*y = y*x \). Then \( * \) is said to be associative if
] for all \( x,y,z \in X , (x*y)*z = x*(y*z) \)

This means that, if we first combine two elements of \( X \), it gives
us an element in \( X \) which we can also combine with another element
in \( X \) to give us an element in \( X \)
Consider the example below

Let \( 3,5,9 \in \mathbb{Z^+}\)
Addition : \( (3+5)+9 = 8+9 =17 \) , also \( 3+(5+9) = 3+14 = 17 \)
We see that \( (3+5)+9 = 3+(5+9) \), so it is associative

Multiplication : \( 3*(5*9)=3*45=135 \) , also \( (3*5)*9 = 15*9 = 135 \)
We see that \( 3*(5*9) = (3*5)*9 \), so it is associative


(4)DISTRIBUTIVE PROPERTY : Let two binary operations ( say \( * , \circ \) )be defined on a non-empty set \( X \) , and
the set \( X \) is closed under the two binary operations. If for all \( x,y,z \in X \),
\( \displaystyle{x*(y \circ z) = (x*y) \circ (x*z)}\) , then the operation \( * \) is said
to be left-distributive over the operation \( \circ \).
If \( \displaystyle{(y \circ z)*x = (y*x) \circ (z*x)}\), then the operation \( * \) is said
to be right-distributive over \( \circ \).

For example
\( \displaystyle{2*(3+4) = 2*7 = 14}\) , also \( \displaystyle{(2*3)+(2*4) = 6+8 = 4}\)
We see that \( \displaystyle{2*(3+4) = (2*3)+(2*4)}\) , so \( * \) is distributive over \( + \) here.


(5)IDENTITY ELEMENT : Let \( X \) be a non-empty set, and let \( * \) be a binary operation on \( X \) such that
(i) \( X \) is closed under \( * \)
(ii) The operation \( * \) is commutative
Then, an element \( e\in X \) is called an identity element
in \( X \) under the operation \( * \) if for all \( x \in X \),
\( \displaystyle{ x*e = e*x = x}\)

If such element \( e \) exist in \( X \), it is always unique. That is, there can be only one identity element.

For example, in the set of positive integers \( \mathbb{Z^+}, 1 \) is the identity element under multiplication.

Check : \( 5*1 = 1*5 =5 \)
\( 100*1 =1*100 =100\)
\( 29*1 = 1*29 = 29\)


(6)INVERSE ELEMENT : Let \( X \) be a non-empty set, and let \( * \) be a binary operation on \( X \) such that
(i) \( X \) is closed under \( * \)
(ii) The operation \( * \) is commutative
(iii) There exist an identity element \( e \in X \) under the operation \( * \)
Then, an element \( y\in X \) is called the inverse of an element \( x \in X \) if
\( \displaystyle{x*y = y*x = e}\)

Note:The inverse of an element is always unique

\( f(p \circ q) = (3p-2) \times (3q-2) \)

\( \implies 9pq-6p-6q+4 \)

\( f(p \circ q) = 9pq-6p-6q+4 \)

\( \implies 3(3pq-2) - 2(3p-2) - 2(3q-2) +2 \)

This means that \( f(pq) = 3pq-2 \) , ...

So we have

\( f(p \circ q) = 3(3pq-2) - 2(3p-2) -2(3q-2) + 2 \)

\( f^{-1}[f(x)] = f^{-1}(3x-2) \implies f^{-1}(3x-2) = x \)

\( f^{-1}[f(p \circ q)] = f^{-1}[3(3pq-2) -2(3p-2)-2(3q-2)+2 ] \)
