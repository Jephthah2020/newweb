MATHEMATICAL INDUCTION



This is a technique that can be used to prove variety of mathematical statements
which are formulated in terms of \( n \), where \( n \) is a positive integer.


The Principle of Mathematical Induction(M.I)



Let \( P(n) \) be the statement involving natural number, \( n \in {\mathbb{N}} \)

STEP 1: prove that \( P(1) \) is true

STEP 2: Assume \( P(k) \) is true when \( n = k \)

STEP 3: prove that \( P(k+1) \) is true, when \( n = {k+1} \)

If it is true from STEP 1 -STEP 3, then \( P(n) \) is true for all \( n \in {\mathbb{N}} \)

To understand this principle, consider the following examples.


Examples



(1) Prove by induction for all \( n \in {\mathbb{N}} \)

\( {1+3+5+...+(2n-1)} = {n^2} \)

Solution

Let \( P(n) \) be the statement. That is

\( P(n) : {1+3+5+...+(2n-1)} = {n^2} \)

STEP 1: we need to show that the statement is true for \( n = 1 \)

Now, we look at the L.H.S and R.H.S of

\( P(n) : {1+3+5+...+(2n-1)} = {n^2} \)

When \( n = 1 \), we need to show that L.H.S \( = \) R.H.S.

For L.H.S

\( {P(1) = {2(1) - 1} = {2-1} = 1 }\)

for R.H.S

\( {P(1) = {(1)^2} = 1 } \)

\( \therefore P(1) \) is true.

STEP 2: Now, we assume \( P(k) \) is true, when \( n = k \). That is

\( P(k) : {1+3+5+...+(2k-1)} = {k^2} \)

STEP 3: Now, we need to prove for \( P(k+1) \), when \( n = {k+1} \).

To do this, we add the next term to the L.H.S

Looking at

\( P(k) : {1+3+5+...+(2k-1)} = {k^2} \)

The next term on the L.H.S when \( k = {k+1} \) is \( {[2(k+1)-1]} \)

So we add \( {[2(k+1)-1]} \) to the L.H.S.

Also on the R.H.S, wherever we see \( k \), we put \( (k+1) \).

Notice on the R.H.S, we have \( k^2 \), so this becomes \( (k+1)^2 \)

Now, we have \( P(k+1) \)

\( P(k+1) : {1+3+5+...+(2k-1)+{[2(k+1)-1]}} = {(k+1)^2} \)

Now we need to prove that \( P(k+1) \) is true.

That is, we need toprove that L.H.S = R.H.S

\( P(k+1) : {1+3+5+...+(2k-1)+{[2(k+1)-1]}} = {(k+1)^2} \)

But recall from STEP 2, that

\( {1+3+5+...+(2k-1)} = {k^2} \)

\( \therefore \) we have

\( P(k+1) : {k^2+{[2(k+1)-1]}} = {(k+1)^2} \)

It remains to show that L.H.S = R.H.S

Now, for L.H.S

L.H.S \( = {k^2+{[2(k+1)-1]}} = {k^2+{[2k+2-1]}} = {k^2+2k+1} \)

\( = {k(k+1)+1(k+1)} = {(k+1)(k+1)} = {(k+1)^2} = \) R.H.S

Therefore, \( P(k+1)\) is also true.

Hence, \( P(n) \) is true for all \( n \in \mathbb{N}\)


(2) Prove by induction for all \( n \in {\mathbb{N}} \), \( n \ge 2 \)

\( { (1-{\frac{1}{2^2}}) \times (1-{\frac{1}{3^2}}) \times ... \times (1-{\frac{1}{n^2}}) } = {\frac{n+1}{2n}} \)

Solution

Let \( P(n) \) be the statement. That is

\( P(n) : { (1-{\frac{1}{2^2}}) \times (1-{\frac{1}{3^2}}) \times ... \times (1-{\frac{1}{n^2}}) } = {\frac{n+1}{2n}} \), \( n \ge 2 \)

STEP 1: we need to show that the statement is true for \( n = 2 \)

Now, we look at the L.H.S and R.H.S of

\( P(n) : { (1-{\frac{1}{2^2}}) \times (1-{\frac{1}{3^2}}) \times ... \times (1-{\frac{1}{n^2}}) } = {\frac{n+1}{2n}} \)

Now when \( n = 2 \) (Since the condition given is \( n \ge 2 \) )

For L.H.S

\( P(2) = {(1-{\frac{1}{2^2}})} = {1 - {\frac{1}{4}}} = {\frac{4-1}{4}} = {\frac{3}{4}} \)

For R.H.S

\( P(2) : {\frac{2+1}{2 \dot 2}} = {\frac{3}{4}} \)

L.H.S = R.H.S

\( \therefore P(2) \) is true

STEP 2: Now, we assume \( P(k) \) is true, when \( n = k \). That is

\( P(k) : { (1-{\frac{1}{2^2}}) \times (1-{\frac{1}{3^2}}) \times ... \times (1-{\frac{1}{k^2}})} = {\frac{k+1}{2k}} \), \( k \ge 2 \)

STEP 3: Now, we need to prove for \( P(k+1) \), when \( n = {k+1} \).

For the L.H.S, we need to input the next term which is \( { (1-{\frac{1}{(k+1)^2}}) } \)

On the R.H.S too, wherever we see \(k \), we put \( {k + 1} \).

That is, the R.H.S becomes \( {\frac{(k+1)+1}{2(k+1)}} \)

That is

\( P(k+1) : { (1-{\frac{1}{2^2}}) \times (1-{\frac{1}{3^2}}) \times ... \times (1-{\frac{1}{k^2}}) \times {(1-{\frac{1}{(k+1)^2}})} } = {\frac{(k+1)+1}{2(k+1)}} \)

So, now, we need to prove that \( P(k+1) \) is true by showing that L.H.S = R.H.S

But recall from STEP 2

\( { (1-{\frac{1}{2^2}}) \times (1-{\frac{1}{3^2}}) \times ... \times (1-{\frac{1}{k^2}})} = {\frac{k+1}{2k}} \)

S0, \( P(k+1) \) becomes

\( P(k+1) : { ({\frac{k+1}{2k}}) \times (1-{\frac{1}{k^2}}) } = {\frac{(k+1)+1}{2(k+1)}} \)

Now, R.H.S \( \implies {\frac{(k+1)+1}{2(k+1)}} = {\frac{k+2}{2(k+1)}} \)

Also, L.H.S \( \implies { ({\frac{k+1}{2k}}) \times (1-{\frac{1}{k^2}}) } = { ({\frac{k+1}{2k}}) \times ({\frac{(k+1)^2-1}{(k+1)^2}}) } \)

\( = { ({\frac{k+1}{2k}}) \times ({\frac{k^2+2k+1-1}{(k+1)^2}}) } \)

\( = { ({\frac{k+1}{2k}}) \times ({\frac{k^2+2k}{(k+1)^2}}) } \)

\( = {\frac{k+1}{2k}} \times {\frac{k(k+2)}{(k+1)^2}} \)

\( = {\frac{k+2}{2(k+1)}} \)

\( \implies \) R.H.S

Since L.H.S = R.H.S

\( P(k+1) \) is true whenever \( P(k) \) is true.

Hence, \( p(n) \) is true \( \forall n \in \mathbb{N} \), \( n \ge 2 \)


(3) Prove by induction

\( {n^2 < (2^n)} \forall n \ge 5 \).

Solution

Let \( P(n) \) be the statement

\( P(n) : {n^2 < (2^n)} \forall n \ge 5 \).

STEP 1: we need to show that the statement is true for \( n = 2 \)

Now, we look at the L.H.S and R.H.S of

\( P(n) : {n^2 < (2^n)} \forall n \ge 5 \).

Now when \( n = 5 \) (Since the condition given is \( n \ge 5 \) )

For L.H.S

\( P(5) : 5^2 = 25 \)

For the L.H.S

\( P(5) : 2^5 = 32 \)

Since \( 25 < 32 \)

\( P(5) \) is true.

STEP 2: Now, we assume \( P(k) \) is true, when \( n = k \). That is

\( P(k) : {k^2 < (2^k)} \forall k \ge 5 \).

STEP 3: Now, we need to prove for \( P(k+1) \), when \( n = {k+1} \).

Wherever we see \( k \) on the L.H.S and R.H.S, we put \( (k+1) \)

\( P(k+1) : {(k+1)^2 < (2^{k+1}) } \).

We need to show that this is true by proving L.H.S < R.H.S.

We prove from L.H.S to R.H.S

L.H.S \( \implies {(k+1)^2 = k^2+2k+1 } \)

Recall from STEP 2 \( P(k) : {k^2 < (2^k)} \)

\( \implies k^2+2k+1 < {2^k+2k+1} \) (since \( {k^2 < (2^k)} \), we substitute \( 2^k \) for \( k^2 \) )

\( < (2^k \times 2) = { 2^{k+1} } \implies \) R.H.S

\( \implies \) L.H.S < R.H.S

Thus, \( P(k+1) \) is true whenever \( P(k) \) is true.

Hence, \( P(n) \) is true \( \forall n \in \mathbb{N} \), \( n \ge 5 \)


(4) Prove by induction

\( { 2n < (n+2)!} \forall n \in \mathbb{N} \)

Solution

Let \( P(n) \) be the statement

\( P(n) : { 2n < (n+2)!} \forall n \in \mathbb{N} \)

STEP 1: we need to show that the statement is true for \( n = 1 \)

On the L.H.S

\( P(1) = 2(1) = 2 \)

On the R.H.S

\( P(1) = { {(1+2)!} = {3!} = 6 } \)

Since \( 2 < 6 \)

\( P(1) \) is true

STEP 2: Now, we assume \( P(k) \) is true, when \( n = k \). That is

\( P(k) : { 2k < (k+2)!} \forall k \in \mathbb{N} \)

STEP 3: Now, we need to prove for \( P(k+1) \), when \( n = {k+1} \).

\( P(k+1) : { 2(k+1) < [(k+1)+2]!} \)

Simplifying, we have

\( P(k+1) : { 2(k+1) < (k+3)!} \)

We need to prove that L.H.S < R.H.S

Now, L.H.S \( \implies {2(k+1)} = {2k+2} \)

Recall from STEP 2, \( { 2k < (k+2)!} \)

\( \implies {2k+2} < {(k+2)! + 2} \)

\( < {(k+3)!} = R.H.S

\( \implies \) L.H.S < {R.H.S}

Thus, \( P(k+1) \) is true whenever \( P(k) \) is true.

Hence, \( P(n) \) is true \( \forall n \in \mathbb{N} \)


(5) Prove by induction

\( {1^2+2^2+...+n^2} = {\frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}} \) , \( \forall n \in \mathbb{N} \)

Solution

Let \( P(n) \) be the statement

\(P(n) : {1^2+2^2+...+n^2} = {\frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}} \) , \( \forall n \in \mathbb{N} \)

STEP 1: we need to show that the statement is true for \( n = 1 \)

On the L.H.S

\( P(1) = (1)^2 = 1 \)

On the R.H.S

\( P(1) = {\frac{1(1+1)(2+1)}{6}} = {\frac{(2)(3)}{6}} = 1 \)

Since L.H.S = R.H.S , \( P(1) \) is true.

STEP 2: Now, we assume \( P(k) \) is true, when \( n = k \). That is

\(P(k) : {1^2+2^2+...+k^2} = {\frac{k(k+1)(2k+1)}{6}} \) , \( \forall k \in \mathbb{N} \)

STEP 3: Now, we need to prove for \( P(k+1) \), when \( n = {k+1} \) .

So, we add the next term to the L.H.S , the next term is \( {(k+1)^2} \) .

Also,on the R.H.S, wherever we see \( k \), we put \( (k+1) \) .

So , R.H.S becomes \( {\frac{(k+1)(k+1+1)[2(k+1)+1]}{6}} = {\frac{(k+1)(k+2)(2k+3)}{6}} \)

So, \(P(k+1) : {1^2+2^2+...+k^2+(k+1)^2} = {\frac{(k+1)(k+2)(2k+3)}{6}} \)

But recall from STEP 2 that \(P(k) : {1^2+2^2+...+k^2} = {\frac{k(k+1)(2k+1)}{6}} \)

So, \( P(k+1) \) becomes

\( P(k+1) : {{\frac{k(k+1)(2k+1)}{6}}+(k+1)^2} = {\frac{(k+1)(k+2)(2k+3)}{6}} \)

We need to prove that this is true by showing that L.H.S = R.H.S

Now, L.H.S \( \implies {{\frac{k(k+1)(2k+1)}{6}}+(k+1)^2} \)

\( = {\frac{k+1}{6}}{[{k(2k+1)+6(k+1)}]} \)

\( = {\frac{k+1}{6}}{[2k^2+k+6k+6]} \)

\( = {\frac{k+1}{6}}{[2k^2+7k+6]} \)

\( = {\frac{k+1}{6}}{[2k^2+4k+3k+6]}\)

\( = {\frac{k+1}{6}}{[2k(k+2)+3(k+2)]} \)

\( = {\frac{k+1}{6}}{[(2k+3)(k+2)]} \)

\( = {\frac{(k+1)(k+2)(2k+3)}{6}} \implies \) R.H.S

Therefore, \( P(k+1) \) is true whenever \( P(k) \) is true

Hence, \( P(n) \) is true \( \forall n \in \mathbb{N} \)

(12) Prove that \( { 6^n - 1} \) is a multiple of \( 5 \) for all values of \( n \)
using the principle of Mathematical Induction.

Solution

Let \( P(n) \) be the statement

\( P(n) : { 6^n - 1} \) is a multiple of \( 5 \)

Now, \( P(1) = {6^1-1} = {6-1} = 5 = {5 \times 1} \)

\( \therefore P(1) \) is true.

Assume \( P(k) \) is true

\( P(k) : { 6^k - 1} = 5M \) , \( M \in \mathbb{Z^+} \)

\( \implies 6^k = {5M+1} \)

Next, we prove that \( P(k+1) \) is true, when \( n = (k+1) \)

\( P(k+1) : {6^{k+1}-1} = {(6 \times 6^k)-1} \)

Recall that \( 6^k = {5M+1} \)

\( \implies P(k+1) = {6[5M+1]-1} = {30M+6-1} \)

\( = {30M+5} = {5(6M+1)} \) which is a multiple of 5.

\( \therefore P(k+1) \) is true whenever \( P(k) \) is true.

Hence \( P(n) \)is true \( \forall n \in \mathbb{Z^+}\)


(13) Show that \( {{18}^n-1} \) is exactly divisible by \( 17 \) , if \( n \) is a positive integer

Solution

Let \( P(n) \) be the statement

\( P(n) : {{18}^n-1} \) is exactly divisible by \( 17 \)

Now, \( P(1) = {{18}^1-1} = {18-1} = 17 = {17 \times 1} \)

\( \therefore P(1) \) is true.

Assume \( P(k) \) is true

\( P(k) : {{18}^k-1} = 17N \) , \( n \in \mathbb{Z^+} \)

\( \implies {18}^k = {17N+1} \)

Next, we prove that \( P(k+1) \) is true, when \( n = (k+1) \)

\( P(k+1) : {{18}^{k+1}-1} \)

\( = {(18 \times {18}^k)-1} \)

\( \implies P(k+1) = {18[{17N+1}]-1} = {(18 \times 17N)+18-1} \)

\( = {(18 \times 17N)+17} = {17[18N+1]} \) which is a multiple of \( 17 \).

\( \therefore P(k+1) \) is true whenever \( P(k) \) is true.

Hence \( P(n) \)is true \( \forall n \in \mathbb{Z^+}\)


(14) Prove that for all positive integer values of \( n \),

(15) Prove that
\( {n^4+4n^2+11} \) is a multiple of \( 6 \) for all odd positive integers.

Solution

Let \( P(n) \) be the statement

\( P(n) : {n^4+4n^2+11} \) is a multiple of \( 6 \) for all odd positive integers.

Now, \( P(1) = {1+4+11} = 16 = {16 \times 1} \)

\( \therefore P(1) \) is true.

Assume \( P(k) \) is true

\( P(k) : {k^4+4k^2+11} = 16M \) ( \(M \in \mathbb{Z^+} \) )

\( \implies k^4 = {16M-4k^2-11} \)

Since \( n \) is odd, we need to prove that \( P(k+2) \) is true, when \( n = (k+2) \)

\( P(k+2) : {(k+2)^4+4(k+2)^2+11} \)

\( = {k^4+8k^3+24k^2+32k+16+4(k^2+4k+4)+11} \)

\( = {k^4+8k^3+28k^2+48k+43} \)

Recall that \( k^4 = {16M-4k^2-11} \)

\( \implies P(k+1) = { [{16M-4k^2-11}]+8k^3+28k^2+48k+43 } \)

\( = {16M+8k^3+24k^2+48k+32} \)

\( = {16[M+{1 \over 4}(2k^3+3k^2)+3k+2]} \)

\( \therefore P(k+2) \) is true whenever \( P(k) \) is true.

Hence, \( P(n) \) is true \( \forall \) odd positive integers.
