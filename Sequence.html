SEQUENCE & SERIES



A sequence is a set of numbers in some definite order,

the successive terms of the sequence being formed according to some rules

Generally, we denote the \( \displaystyle{r^{th}} \) term of a sequence by \( \displaystyle{U_r} \).

For example, the sequence

\( \displaystyle{2,4,6,8,10, \cdots} \)

canbe denoted by \( \displaystyle{U_r} \). But how can we find \( \displaystyle{U_r} \)?

By inspection, we see that in the sequence

\( \displaystyle{2,4,6,8,10, \cdots} \)

the terms can be written as

\( \displaystyle{{2 \times 1},{2 \times 2},{2 \times 3},{2 \times 4},{2 \times 5}, \cdots} \)

Therefore, \( \displaystyle{U_r = 2r} \) where \( \displaystyle{r \in \mathbb{Z^+}} \) (positive integer)


Series: A series is obtained by forming from the sum of the terms of a sequence.

A finite series is obtained if a finite number of terms of the sequence are summed.

We denote the sum of the first \( n \) terms ofa sequence by \( \displaystyle{S_n} \), where

\( \displaystyle{S_n = u_1+u_2+u_3+ \cdots u_n} \)

or we write

\( \displaystyle{\sum\limits_{r=1}^{n}u_r = u_1+u_2+u_3+ \cdots u_n } \)

Note( \( \displaystyle{ \sum \limits_{r=1}^{n}} \) means summation from \( r=1 \) to \( r=n \) )

Examples

(1) Find a formula for \( \displaystyle{U_r} \) for the sequences

(a) \( \displaystyle{1,8,27,64,125, \cdots} \)

(b) \( \displaystyle{1,-4, 9, -16, 25, \cdots } \)

(c) \( \displaystyle{{1 \over 2}, 2 , 8 , 32, 128, \cdots} \)


Solution

(a) \( \displaystyle{1,8,27,64,125, \cdots} \)

By inspection, the terms can be terms can be written as

\( \displaystyle{1^3, 2^3, 3^3, 4^3, 5^3, \cdots } \)

\( \displaystyle{\therefore U_r = r^3} \)

(b) \( \displaystyle{1,-4, 9, -16, 25, \cdots } \)

By inspection, the terms can be written as

\( \displaystyle{{(-1)^{1+1}(1)^2},{(-1)^{2+1}(2)^2},{(-1)^{3+1}(3)^2},{(-1)^{4+1}(4)^2}, \cdots } \)

\( \displaystyle{\therefore U_r = {(-1)^{r+1}r^2}} \)


(c) \( \displaystyle{{1 \over 2}, 2 , 8 , 32, 128, \cdots} \)

By inspection, the terms can be written as

\( \displaystyle{{{1 \over 2} \times 1},{{1 \over 2} \times 4},{{1 \over 2} \times {16}},{{1 \over 2} \times {64}},{{1 \over 2} \times {256}}, \cdots } \)

This can be written as

\( \displaystyle{{{1 \over 2} \times {(4)^{1-1}}},{{1 \over 2} \times {(4)^{2-1}}},{{1 \over 2} \times {(4)^{3-1}}},{{1 \over 2} \times {(4)^{4-1}}},{{1 \over 2} \times {(4)^{5-1}}}, \cdots } \)

\( \displaystyle{\therefore U_r = {({1\over 2})4^{r-1}}} \)

Arithmetric Progression(A.P)

An arithmetic series is defined by its first term and common

difference denoted by \( a \) and \( d \) respectively.

The general Arithmetic sequence is

\( \displaystyle{a, a+d, a+2d, a+3d, a+4d, \cdots , a+(n-1)d, \cdots} \)

In generaL, the \( nth \) term of an Arithmetic progression is given by

\( \displaystyle{U_n = a+(n-1)d} \)

The sum of the first n terms of an Arithmetic progerssion is given by

\( \displaystyle{S_n = \frac{n}{2}[2a+(n-1)d]} \)

Or

\( \displaystyle{S_n = {n \over 2}[a+l]} \)

where \( a \) is the first term,

\( d \) is the common difference,

\( l \) is the last term.


Examples

(1). Find \( 12 \) Arithmetic means between \( -5 \) and \( 60 \).

Solution


Given that the first term is \( -5 \) and the \( nth \) term is \( 60 \).

we are asked to find \( 12 \) arithmetic means between them.

This shows that the A.P has \(14 \) terms, where

the first term is \( -5 \) and the last term is \( 60 \).

Now, the first term \( \displaystyle{U_1 = a = -5} \)

The \( 14th \) term \( \displaystyle{U_{14} = a+13d = 60} \)

We have two equations

\( \displaystyle{a = -5} \) (1)

\( \displaystyle{a+13d = 60} \)(2)

This gives \( \displaystyle{d = 5} \)

Since \( \displaystyle{U_n = a+(n-1)d} \), the arithmetic means are

\( \displaystyle{0,5,10,15,20,25,30,35,40,45,50,55} \)


(2) Find three numbers in A.P whose sum is \( 3 \) and whose product is \( -15 \)

Solution

Let the numbers be \( \displaystyle{a-d, a, a+d} \)

Given that the sum is \( 3 \) and the product is \( -15 \),

we have

\( \displaystyle{(a-d)+a+(a+d)= 3} \)

\( \displaystyle{\implies 3a = 3 \implies a=1} \)

Also, the product

\( \displaystyle{a(a-d)(a+d)= -15} \)

\( \displaystyle{\implies a(a^2-d^2) = -15} \)

\( \displaystyle{\implies a^3-ad^2 = -15} \)

Since \( a =1 \) , we have

\( \displaystyle{1-d^2 =-15} \)

\( \displaystyle{\implies d^2 = 18} \)

\( \displaystyle{\implies d= \sqrt{16}= 4} \).

So, \( \displaystyle{a=1, d=4} \)

The numbers are \( \displaystyle{a-d, a, a+d, } \) , that is

\( \displaystyle{-3, 1 , 5} \)


(3) The first term of an Arithmetic Progression is \( 7 \), the last term is \( 70 \) , and the sum is \( 385 \) .

Solution

\( \displaystyle{a=7} \)

\( \displaystyle{U_n = a+(n-)d} \)

Geometric Progression(G.P)



A geometric sequence is completely defined byits first term denoted

by \( a \) , and the common ratio denoted by \( r \).

The general geometric sequence is

\( \displaystyle{a,ar,ar^3,ar^3, \cdots , ar^{n-1}} \)

So, the \( nth \)term ofthe sequence is given by

\( \displaystyle{U_n = ar^{n-1}} \)

The sum of the first \( n \) terms ofa geometric sequence is given by

\( \displaystyle{S_n = \frac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r}} \) , if \( r <1 \)

Or

\( \displaystyle{S_n = \frac{a(r^n-1)}{r-1}} \) , if \( r>1 \)

Infinite Geometric Series



The sum to infinity of a G.P canbe derived from the formula

\( \displaystyle{S_n = \frac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r}} \)

\( \displaystyle{\implies \frac{a-ar^n}{1-r}} \)

\( \displaystyle{\implies \frac{a}{1-r} - \frac{ar^n}{1-r}} \)

\( \displaystyle{\implies S_n = \frac{a}{1-r}- a \frac{r^n}{1-r}} \)

As \( \displaystyle{n \to \infty , r^n \to 0} \) and therefore, wehave

\( \displaystyle{S_{\infty} = \frac{a}{1-r} -0} \)

\( \displaystyle{\implies S_{\infty} = \frac{a}{1-r}} \) (the sum to infinity)


Examples

Find the sum to infinity of the series

(a) \( \displaystyle{{1 \over 5}+{1 \over {25}}+{1 \over {125}}+{1\over {625}}+ \cdots } \)

(b) \( \displaystyle{{1 \over 6}+{{1\over 6}\left({5 \over 6}\right)^2}+{{1 \over 6}\left({5 \over 6}\right)^4}+ \cdots } \)


Solution


(a) \( \displaystyle{{1 \over 5}+{1 \over {25}}+{1 \over {125}}+{1\over {625}}+ \cdots } \)

here, \( \displaystyle{a= {1 \over 5}} \cdots \) (1)

also, \( \displaystyle{ar=\frac{1}{25}} \cdots \) (2)

Dividing (2) by (1)

\( \displaystyle{r = {1 \over {25}} \div {1 \over 5} = {1 \over 5}} \)

\( \displaystyle{\therefore r= {1 \over 5}} \)

The sum to infinity

\( \displaystyle{S_{\infty} = \frac{1 \over 5}{1 - {1 \over 5}}} \)

\( \displaystyle{\implies \frac{1}{5} \div \frac{4}{5}} \)

\( \displaystyle{\implies S_{\infty}= {1 \over 4}} \)


(b) \( \displaystyle{{1 \over 6}+{{1\over 6}\left({5 \over 6}\right)^2}+{{1 \over 6}\left({5 \over 6}\right)^4}+ \cdots } \)

here, \( \displaystyle{a = {1 \over 6}} \cdots \) (1)

also, \( \displaystyle{ar={1 \over 6}\left({5 \over 6}\right)^2} \cdots \) (2)

dividing (2) by (1)

\( \displaystyle{r = {1 \over 6}\left({5 \over 6}\right)^2 \div {1 \over 6}} \)

\( \displaystyle{\implies r = \left({5 \over 6}\right)^2} \)

\( \displaystyle{\implies r= \frac{25}{36}} \)

The sum to infinity

\( \displaystyle{S_{\infty} = \frac{a}{1-r} = \frac{1 \over 6}{1-{\frac{25}{36}}}} \)

\( \displaystyle{\implies {1 \over 6} \div \frac{11}{36}} \)

\( \displaystyle{\implies {1 \over 6} \times {\frac{36}{11}}} \)

\( \displaystyle{\implies S_{\infty} = \frac{6}{11}} \)
